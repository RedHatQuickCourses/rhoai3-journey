Making RHOAI Data Connections and Storage Work for You

Architecting AI Storage Logistics in OpenShift AI

Connectivity is easy. Performance is engineering.

In the early stages of AI adoption, the primary challenge for Platform Engineers was simply access: "How do I connect my data scientist’s Jupyter Notebook to an S3 bucket without exposing the root password?"

But as your organization moves from a "Science Project" to a "Production Service," the challenges shift dramatically. You are no longer moving 1GB CSV files; you are serving 70GB Large Language Models (LLMs) to thousands of concurrent users, and automating complex, multi-stage AI Pipelines.

At this scale, storage is no longer just a place to keep files—it becomes the primary bottleneck of your entire platform.

The Cost of "Shadow AI" Storage

When data scientists operate outside of a governed architecture, they default to what is easiest. While fine for a laptop, this ad-hoc "Shadow AI" approach is disastrous for production Kubernetes clusters and destroys your GPU Return on Investment (ROI).

  • The Network Flood: If every pod downloads a massive foundation model on startup to a local ephemeral disk (emptyDir), you trigger severe network saturation and node bandwidth exhaustion.

  • The Egress Cost: If your automated AI pipelines pass massive intermediate datasets back and forth to an external S3 bucket between every single task, your cloud egress bills explode and your pipeline execution crawls to a halt.

  • The Uptime Threat: If you rely on ephemeral storage for production serving, a single node reboot can take down your inference service for 20 minutes while it re-downloads assets. Every minute your expensive GPUs sit idle waiting for a storage download is burned capital.

The Three Pillars of the AI Storage Factory

To build a stable platform, you must respect the physics of the underlying infrastructure. In this course, we replace the manual, "One-Size-Fits-All" storage approach with a tiered, GitOps-driven strategy specifically engineered for OpenShift AI 3.x workloads.

The Pillar The Shadow AI Problem The Factory Solution

The Velocity Tier

Initialization Latency: Waiting 15 minutes for a model to download over the network before a pod becomes "Ready".

Boot-from-Registry (OCI Modelcars): By packaging models as OCI images, you leverage the cluster’s native container runtime (CRI-O) cache. Scaling from 1 to 50 replicas takes seconds, not hours, because the data layers are already on the node.

The Persistence Tier

S3 Ping-Ponging: Constantly uploading and downloading intermediate datasets between tasks causes severe latency and high cloud costs.

Dual-Storage Pipelines: OpenShift AI 3.3 introduces a hybrid architecture. We use S3 strictly as the permanent archive for logs/artifacts, paired with high-speed Persistent Volume Claims (PVCs) for ephemeral workspaces. Data moves directly via the local cluster disk.

The Abstraction Tier

Fragile Governance: Hardcoded AWS keys in Jupyter notebooks and manual UI configuration.

Kubernetes-Native Abstraction: We utilize the Connections API to inject standard URIs (s3://, pvc://, oci://) via Kubernetes Secrets. Your entire data foundation is managed by GitOps (e.g., Argo CD), ensuring perfect environment parity.
The Shift in Mindset
We are stopping the practice of treating models like loose "files." In the industrialized AI Factory, we treat models, datasets, and pipelines like software artifacts—versioned, immutable, and natively integrated into the Kubernetes control plane. Your mission is to build the Data Supply Chain. In the next sections, we will architect the highway, secure the vault, and automate deployment using provided scripts.
