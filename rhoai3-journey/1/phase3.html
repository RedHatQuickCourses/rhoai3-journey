Phase 3: Scaling the Utility

Technology Preview Notice

Models-as-a-Service (MaaS) is currently available in Red Hat OpenShift AI 3.3 as a Technology Preview feature. While we are actively building out these capabilities to drive business value, they are not yet supported with production service level agreements (SLAs). This phase is designed to equip you with a GitOps framework to build proofs-of-concept and understand the architecture ahead of General Availability (GA).

If you’ve followed the rest of this OpenShift AI series, you already know how to serve a model. But here’s the reality check from the field: simply hosting a model isn’t enough anymore.

In Phase 2, we built a highly tuned, single-model production engine. However, if every development team in your organization demands their own dedicated engine, your infrastructure will collapse. The old "ticket-based" workflow—where developers request dedicated GPUs for every isolated experiment—is a severe operational bottleneck. It leads to idle hardware, massive cloud bills, and "Shadow AI" where developers bypass IT to use public APIs, leaking proprietary enterprise data in the process.

In Phase 3, we scale the utility. With the release of Red Hat OpenShift AI 3.3, we have a strategic answer: Models as a Service (MaaS). We are going to stop treating AI like a series of isolated science projects and start running it like an industrialized factory.

Part 1: The Internal Token Provider (MaaS)

When we architect this solution, our goal is to help the business shift from consuming public AI to becoming an internal AI provider.

  • Sovereign AI & Data Privacy: Use cases that cannot run on public services due to privacy and regulatory concerns can now be served locally. By hosting validated open-source models centrally on OpenShift, no proprietary data ever leaves the hybrid environment.

  • Accelerated Enterprise Adoption: By removing the infrastructure bottleneck, developers no longer wait weeks for GPU provisioning. They simply log into the Gen AI Studio, request an API key, and instantly integrate governed LLM endpoints into their applications.

Part 2: Maximizing ROI with Distributed Inference

Dedicated hardware per project destroys ROI. We must move away from standalone serving when dealing with enterprise-wide demand.

By pooling resources using Distributed Inference (llm-d), rate-limiting, and quota management, we build a highly utilized "token generation factory at scale". The business serves the entire organization from a consolidated, cost-efficient hardware footprint.

Part 3: The Invisible Dependency Chain

To successfully deliver this at scale, we have to bridge the gap between two distinct personas: the Platform Engineer (Infrastructure) and the AI Administrator (Business Logic).

You will learn how to build the "Invisible Dependency Chain" that makes MaaS possible:

  • Gateway API: For intelligent traffic routing.

  • Kuadrant: For authentication and strict rate-limiting to prevent noisy-neighbor disruptions.

  • Kueue: For enterprise resource quotas.

  • llm-d: For high-efficiency caching and distributed workload balancing.

The Engineering Outcome

Because this is the capstone of our RHOAI series, we are taking a GitOps-First approach. While OpenShift AI 3.3 introduces a beautiful UI, true enterprise scale requires Infrastructure-as-Code.

  • Checkpoint: By the end of this phase, you will deploy the prerequisites via YAML, enable the MaaS backend, configure the Gateway, and apply RBAC roles for tier-based governance. You will also master the Gen AI Studio dashboard to modify default Free/Premium Service Tiers, map user groups, deploy models visually, and generate API keys.

Once you are successfully providing governed API endpoints to your developers, we will secure the outputs and enable autonomous workflows in Phase 4: Governance & Compound Systems.
