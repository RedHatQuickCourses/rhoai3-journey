Lab 4: Scaling the Utility (llm-d & MaaS)

A single GPU is a bottleneck. A distributed, governed network of GPUs is an AI Factory.

As you progress toward Phase 3: Scaling the Utility in your learning journey, the requirements of your token engine will change drastically. You will no longer be serving small, quantized models on single nodes. You will be asked to serve massive, 70B+ parameter models that require sharding across multiple physical GPUs or even multiple nodes.

Furthermore, you will need to expose these massive models as governed APIs (Models-as-a-Service) to internal development teams.

In this final advanced lab, you will use your Web Terminal to deploy the operators required for Distributed Inference (llm-d) and verify your MaaS gateway readiness.

1. The Distributed Engine: Leader Worker Set Operator

Standard KServe deployments use a single Pod to run a model. Distributed Inference (llm-d) breaks this paradigm. It shards a massive model (like Llama 3 70B) across multiple "Worker" pods that continuously communicate with a single "Leader" pod to generate a single, unified response.

Kubernetes does not natively understand this complex Leader/Worker orchestration. To unlock this capability, we must deploy the Red Hat Leader Worker Set Operator.

Procedure: Apply the Operator Manifest

Ensure your Web Terminal (>_) is open. Deploy the operator globally by applying the following Subscription:

cat <<EOF | oc apply -f -
apiVersion: operators.coreos.com/v1alpha1
kind: Subscription
metadata:
  name: leader-worker-set
  namespace: openshift-operators
spec:
  channel: stable
  name: leader-worker-set
  source: redhat-operators
  sourceNamespace: openshift-marketplace
EOF

Verification & Value Context

Verify the installation using the CLI:

oc get csv -n openshift-operators | grep leader-worker-set

  • The GenAI Value: Without this operator, you cannot scale horizontally. By installing the Leader Worker Set, you enable vLLM to shard its tensor parallel (TP) and pipeline parallel (PP) operations across your entire GPU fleet, drastically increasing your token throughput and lowering latency for massive models.

2. Verifying the MaaS Gateway (Kuadrant)

In Lab 2, you installed the Red Hat Connectivity Link Operator. This operator is the secret weapon for Models-as-a-Service (MaaS).

By deploying it, you injected the Kuadrant engine into your cluster. Kuadrant attaches directly to the OpenShift Gateway API. Instead of application traffic hitting your GPU directly, it must first pass through this gateway, where Kuadrant evaluates your custom AuthPolicy (API Keys) and RateLimitPolicy (Tokens per Minute).

Procedure: Verify Kuadrant Readiness

While the actual configuration of MaaS policies will be covered deeply in the rhoai3-maas module, you must ensure the foundation is ready.

Check that the Connectivity Link operator has successfully instantiated the Kuadrant control plane:

oc get pods -n kuadrant-system

(Note: Depending on your specific OpenShift AI 3.x initialization sequence, Kuadrant may deploy in kuadrant-system or be managed directly within the redhat-ods-applications gateway space).

  • The GenAI Value: This infrastructure prevents a single "noisy neighbor" development team from crashing your inference nodes with runaway scripts. It allows the business to offer tiered access (Free vs. Premium SLAs) and internally bill departments for exact token consumption.

You have now completely mapped the required OpenShift AI infrastructure to the business journey:

  1. Phase 1 (The Factory): Powered by the Red Hat OpenShift AI Operator and NFD.

  2. Phase 2 (The Engine): Secured by the cert-manager Operator.

  3. Phase 3 (Scaled Utility): Distributed by the Leader Worker Set and Governed by Connectivity Link.

You are fully prepared to tackle the advanced deployment methodologies in the llmops-llmd and rhoai3-maas modules.
