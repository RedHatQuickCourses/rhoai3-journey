Operators & Ecosystem

Your business mandate is to flip that narrative: to move from consuming tokens to producing them at scale.

To successfully build and troubleshoot this AI Factory, a Platform Engineer must understand the underlying ecosystem. Deploying every operator in the catalog is an anti-pattern: you pay in CPU, memory, reconciliation storms, and upgrade risks. Instead, we provision strictly against our factory outcomes.

1. The Meta-Operator & Namespace Isolation

A common misconception is that installing the OpenShift AI operator immediately spins up your entire factory. Instead, it relies on the Operator Lifecycle Manager (OLM) and a Meta-Operator Pattern.

When installed, the Red Hat OpenShift AI Operator acts as the factory manager. It waits for you to define your DataScienceCluster custom resource (which we will do in Lab 3), and then it orchestrates the deployment of specific components across isolated namespaces.

Familiarize yourself with these namespaces, as you will spend a lot of time troubleshooting within them:

  • redhat-ods-operator: The control room. Where the meta-operator itself lives and runs its reconciliation loops.

  • redhat-ods-applications: The factory floor. Where the core UI, dashboard components, and Model Catalog run.

  • rhoai-model-registries: The isolated namespace specifically targeted for the Model Registry components (think of this as your AI "Title Office").

  • rhods-notebooks: The R&D lab. Where standard data science workbenches are deployed by default.

2. The Dependency Rules: Why Order Matters

Generative AI at scale is a highly complex compound system. You cannot install components out of order and expect the factory to run. The following dependency chain dictates our installation progression:

Hardware Labels before Hardware Allocation

GPUs you cannot label are GPUs you cannot schedule reliably. You must install Node Feature Discovery (NFD) to publish hardware labels (CPU flags, PCI devices, accelerators) on nodes. Only once NFD has detected the hardware can the GPU operators properly attach drivers so Kubernetes can schedule resources.limits on GPUs.

Trust before Traffic

Unsecured inference endpoints are a liability. KServe-oriented stacks commonly depend on in-cluster certificate issuance. The cert-manager Operator must be installed before the OpenShift AI operator to eliminate manual certificate rotation theater.

Gateways before Governed Exposure

An unmanaged endpoint is a liability; a governed endpoint is a product. To enable Models-as-a-Service (MaaS) with strict authentication and rate-limiting, the gateway infrastructure must be present.

The Evolution of Auth: Authorino vs. Connectivity Link

If you deployed older 2.x environments, you likely installed the Authorino Operator directly. For OpenShift AI 3.3 and the new Gateway API MaaS patterns, the Red Hat Connectivity Link Operator is your unified gateway manager. It installs the Kuadrant-oriented connectivity layer, handling the underlying authentication (JWT validation) and rate-limiting policies previously managed by standalone Authorino deployments.

3. The Platform Engineer’s Decision Matrix

Use the following matrices to understand exactly which operators unlock which capabilities in our journey. We have structured this into the exact order you should deploy them.

A: The Core Pre-requisites (Install First)

These operators lay the foundation. They must be present and healthy before the OpenShift AI meta-operator is installed.

Operator What it does Factory Outcome

Node Feature Discovery (NFD)

Scans the cluster and publishes hardware labels on nodes.

GPU operators and schedulers see the same truth. Hardware placement stops being guesswork.

cert-manager

Provides issuers, certificates, and renewals for in-cluster TLS consumers.

Strict Prerequisite: Secures KServe and distributed workloads. Eliminates manual cert rotation.

Hardware Accelerator Operator

Manages drivers and device plugins for your specific hardware.

Enables GPU-as-a-Service. For this use case, we will use the NVIDIA GPU Operator. (Alternatives: AMD GPU, Intel Gaudi Base, or IBM Spyre Operators).

Red Hat build of Kueue

Controls quotas, priorities, and preemption policies.

Strict Prerequisite for Hardware Profiles. Stops queue sprawl and aligns compute spend with business priority.

B: The Meta-Operator & Automated Subsystems

Once the concrete is poured, we install the factory manager.

Operator What it does Factory Outcome

Red Hat OpenShift AI Operator

The core meta-operator that manages the entire AI lifecycle platform.

Waits for your DataScienceCluster blueprint to deploy the dashboard, model registry, and serving controllers. (Requires v3.3+ for MaaS).

Service Mesh 3 Operator

Manages pod-to-pod encrypted traffic, telemetry, and tracing.

Auto-Managed: In v3.3, the OpenShift AI operator automatically handles the installation/integration of Service Mesh 3 when required by components like the Llama Stack.

C: Inference at Scale & MaaS (Scale-Out Machinery)

To deliver Large Language Models (LLMs) as a governed, shared resource across your enterprise, these operators handle distributed serving and external access.

Operator What it does Factory Outcome

Red Hat Connectivity Link

Provides Kuadrant-style policy attachment, authN/Z hooks, and rate limits on ingress.

Turns raw endpoints into products with SLAs—internal MaaS without anonymous GPU burn.

Leader Worker Set

Orchestration primitive for leader/worker distributed pods.

Required for Distributed Inference (llm-d). Lets very large models span devices and nodes instead of forcing vertical-only designs.
MaaS Configuration Target

Operators and this foundation are critical; they make the difference between a feature working and failing silently. To optimally configure your generative AI platform for MaaS, you will eventually set modelsAsService to Managed and kserve to Managed in your DataScienceCluster custom resource. Do not attempt this until the roster above is successfully running.
